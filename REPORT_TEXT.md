## 02 / An Honest Read of the Data

What the survey is really telling us.

Signal: charging is a retention amenity

More than half of respondents said EV chargers would make them more likely to keep living at Aperture. One resident specifically tied charging expansion to renewal, saying confirmation of more charging stations would affect their decision to renew for another year.

This is the strongest business case in the survey: charging is not only a sustainability feature, it is part of the leasing and renewal decision for current residents.

Signal: execution details matter

The concerns are practical and solvable. Residents raised fair-access queues, connector compatibility, the current wait for chargers, and avoiding premium spaces near elevators.

A credible recommendation should address charger type, access control, pricing, queueing, stall location, and whether charging spaces are reserved only while actively charging.

EV charging was actually the deciding factor for my decision to sign a lease with Aperture.

- Aperture resident, current EV charging user

## 03 / Deployment Sizing

A practical starting point: 8 to 10 additional Level 2 ports.

The historical one-property model used one charging port for roughly every two current EV/PHEV residents. Applying that same rule to Aperture's 19 current EV/PHEV respondents indicates a 10-port current-demand target. If the existing two charging cables remain in service, the incremental planning range is approximately 8 additional ports.

Current Demand Rule

10 ports

19 current EV/PHEV owners divided by two residents per charging port, rounded up.

Existing Amenity

2 ports

Resident comments repeatedly reference the current two chargers or charging cables as insufficient.

Incremental Need

8 ports

Initial planning target if the existing charging setup remains available and reliable.

Recommended charger profile

Use networked Level 2 hardware with managed charging, access controls, and queue visibility. Connector choice should support both Tesla and non-Tesla residents; Dominion's program materials reference ChargePoint and Tesla options, and any owner-procured station must be able to charge any make/model of EV.

Location and policy priorities

Place chargers where electrical work is efficient without converting the most sensitive premium spaces near elevators unless management deliberately chooses that tradeoff. Enforce active-charging rules, resident access controls, and pricing that discourages overstays while still supporting cost recovery.

What still needs to be confirmed

Final charger count, charger model, install cost, electrical capacity, garage routing, hardware cost, network fees, and Dominion eligibility require a site walk, charger selection, and a completed Dominion calculator. The current report treats 8 to 10 additional ports as a planning range, not a construction quote.

## 05 / Program Economics

Dominion Energy can change the project math.

Dominion Energy's Virginia Level 2 Charging Program provides a 50% upfront incentive on eligible EV charging make-ready construction and installation, with the remaining eligible costs paid through monthly on-bill payments. Aperture's current calculator is useful, but incomplete because hardware cost is still marked unknown.

Why this matters for Aperture

The largest variable in a garage charging project is often the electrical work before chargers are installed: panels, conduit, wiring, routing, metering, and related make-ready infrastructure. Dominion's program is directly relevant because it targets that make-ready cost category.

Program structure

50% make-ready incentive

Dominion describes the incentive as applying to EV charging construction and installation, commonly referred to as make-ready.

Minimum two qualifying stations

Eligibility generally requires a Virginia commercial or industrial Dominion customer, decision authority for the site, at least two qualifying stations, a program contract, and site readiness.

ChargePoint and Tesla options

Dominion's program materials reference ChargePoint and Tesla options when Dominion installs and maintains the charging stations.

Customer control of use and pricing

Site hosts can set access controls and usage fees through the charging provider, which lets Aperture manage resident access, overstays, and cost recovery.

Program description summarized from Dominion Energy's public Level 2 Charging Program materials. Final eligibility, pricing, approved hardware, and contract terms should be confirmed with Dominion Energy before approval.

## 06 / Recommendation

A measured path forward.

Proposed next step

Design a resident-first expansion of the existing charging amenity.

The data supports moving forward, but the financial model should not be presented as final until charger selection and site design are complete. The right next step is a scoped site and program-design pass that converts the survey signal into a final port count, hardware recommendation, Dominion submission, and investment model.

I.

Confirm Site Design

Validate garage electrical capacity, routing, meter requirements, station locations, and whether the existing two charging ports remain in the operating plan.

II.

Select Hardware

Compare ChargePoint, Tesla Universal, and any other Dominion-qualified options for connector compatibility, queueing, access control, pricing, network fees, and resident experience.

III.

Finalize NOI Model

Complete the Dominion calculator with hardware cost, final port count, utilization assumptions, fees, electricity tariff, retention assumptions, and management's target pricing policy.
